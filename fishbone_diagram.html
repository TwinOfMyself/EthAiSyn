Root Cause Analysis · Ishikawa Diagram

Why does a promised callback fall through the cracks?

Analysing the system failure behind an unmet commitment — and why one broken promise undoes weeks of trust-building.

Problem Statement

Case Manager identifies an urgent medication need, promises a callback to the patient, and never follows through — leaving the patient without resolution and eroding trust in the care system. Mr. Tan's case: Friday call, end-of-day callback promised, never received.

Cause Categories — Detailed Breakdown
People (Manpower)
CM is overloaded with active cases No capacity to follow through on each promise made
No designated backup when CM is unavailable Commitment lives only in one person's memory
Process (Methods)
No open-commitment tracking system Verbal promises exist only in the CM's memory
No end-of-day handover protocol Unresolved items are not handed off before close
Commitments not logged in any system No audit trail — promise is invisible to the team
Machines / Technology
No shared cross-team task or ticketing system Inpatient and outpatient teams use separate tools
EMR cannot flag pending callbacks System has no "open loop" status for patient contacts
No automated reminder to CM Nothing prompts the CM to revisit the promise
Materials / Information
Prescribing doctor unreachable after hours CM cannot resolve the medication issue even if they try
Pharmacy or on-call contact info not readily available No fallback path to fulfil the promise independently
Measurement
No service-level agreement for callback turnaround No standard that defines "how fast is fast enough"
No alert triggered if promise remains unresolved Broken promises are invisible — never counted or reported
Environment
Late Friday timing — staff in wind-down mode Mr. Tan's call came at the worst possible moment
Weekend coverage gap Even if flagged Friday, no one acts until Monday
Competing urgent cases in the same window CM is pulled away before the callback can happen
★ Root Cause (Priority Finding)

The system has no mechanism to track open commitments. When a Case Manager makes a verbal promise to a patient, that commitment exists only in one person's memory — with no log, no alert, no escalation path, and no handover protocol. When the CM is diverted by other urgent cases or fails to reach the prescribing doctor, the promise silently falls through with no one catching it. The failure is not malicious intent — it is a predictable outcome of a system that never designed for promise accountability. One unmet callback at a critical juncture (Friday, end-of-day, urgent medication) becomes the pivot point at which the patient concludes the system is unreliable — erasing weeks of potential trust-building.
